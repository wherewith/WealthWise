What is a FICO Score?

A FICO score (A.K.A. credit score) is like a report card for your money- Just like how your report card shows how well you do in school, a FICO score shows how well you handle money and pay back loans. FICO Scores range from 300 to 850 based on how trustworthy the bank considers you to be. It is a measure of your payment history, amounts owed, credit length, inquiries, and credit mix.

Why does it matter?

If you are applying for a new credit card or trying to take out a loan, lenders can quickly assess your credit history using your FICO score. If your FICO score is too low, it may be more difficult for you to get approved for loans or credit cards, and if you are approved, you may be offered worse terms, such as higher interest rates. This can make it more expensive to borrow money, and can also limit your to make large purchases, such as a car or a house. Additionally, landlords and employers may also check your credit score as a part of their background check process, so a low score could make it harder for you to rent an apartment or get a job, since FICO scores are a measure of how responsible you are with your money.

How do I improve my score?

To improve your FICO score, here are some categories to consider:

Make sure to pay your lender(s) on time, every time. In fact, 35% of your FICO score is from this category. This makes up the largest component as it shows that you are a trustable and responsible candidate when it comes to taking loans.

Revolving utilization is calculated as a percentage of the balance you owe out of your total credit line. It is suggested to keep the credit utilization at 30% or less whenever possible. This makes up 30% of your FICO score.

The longer your credit history, the better. Naturally, with time, the score contribution of this section will increase. A good credit length is considered to be 7 years. This makes up 15% of your FICO score. It is still important that you are making credit payments on-time.

Having multiple credit types shows that you are responsible, which improves your FICO score. On top of that, this can help lower your revolving utilization. This makes up 10% of your FICO score.

When you apply for credit, the lender will perform what is known as a hard inquiry (credit check). This will temporarily reduce your credit score, and stay for 24 months. This makes up 10% of your FICO score.
